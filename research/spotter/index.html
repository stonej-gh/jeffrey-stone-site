Research / the eye

Experiment 02

The net that watches the screen.

The first experiment's pilot got its senses served on a plate — nineteen tidy, hand-engineered numbers. Real systems don't get that gift: they get pixels. This experiment closes the gap. A 28,000-parameter convolutional network watches raw game frames and paints what it understands — every ship, every laser, the black hole — live, while the game plays. Then it gets squeezed into 8-bit integers, and you get to try to tell the difference. Perception and policy: the two halves of an autonomy stack, both small enough to read.

Same rules as the pilot's notebook: the plain story reads on its own; ▸ FOR THE LEARNERS rebuilds the machinery from engineering ground — this experiment is the more analog-flavored of the two, all sampling grids and dynamic range; ▸ FOR THE DATA SCIENTISTS is the working notebook, with references to go deeper.

01 · The clip — thirty seconds, one switch to spot

Blue — interceptor Orange — fighter Yellow — laser Magenta — the hole

A duel the network never saw during training, replayed while the network watches. First the raw frames; then the overlay — the net's understanding, painted over every pixel it classifies; then the switch: the same network converted to 8-bit integer arithmetic, alternating with the original every second and a half. Watch for the difference. (On a busy frame it's two pixels out of 61,440.)

One honesty note: nothing you're watching runs in a deep-learning framework. Every painted pixel comes from the project's frozen, shippable file of numbers, through a reference implementation short enough to read — the same artifact a chip would run. More on that in chapter 07.

02 · The eye

The whole eye is 28,000 numbers.

The pilot's brain was 6,220 numbers; the eye is about 28,000 — big enough to see, still small enough to print. It's a convolutional network: instead of looking at all 61,440 pixels at once, it sweeps small learned patterns across the image, the same trick your visual cortex opens with. Feed it a raw 320×192 frame and it answers, for every single pixel: background, interceptor, fighter, laser, or hole.

The two ships get told apart by shape, not color — the classes were designed so the narrow dart and the broad wedge differ in silhouette, and the test set proves the net isn't just color-matching. The black hole's glowing halo is deliberately labeled background: the eye learns that only the dark disk at the center kills you. The glow is scenery.

For the learners

You already know convolution — this is your convolution, the sliding dot product from signals, in 2-D. A conv layer holds a bank of tiny FIR kernels (here 3×3) and slides each across the image; the output at each position is the correlation of kernel and local patch. Everything you know transfers: it's shift-invariant (a translated ship produces a translated response — that's why vision nets are built this way), and stacking layers grows the receptive field, the input footprint that can influence one output — the spatial support of the composed kernel. The differences from DSP: kernels are learned, not designed; each layer runs many kernels in parallel producing channels (a vector of local features per position, like I/Q but 32-wide); each kernel sees all input channels; and a squashing nonlinearity sits between layers — here ReLU, max(0,·), an ideal rectifier — so the stack can't collapse into one linear filter.

Stride is decimation: a stride-2 conv computes its output at every second position — downsampling by two, exactly your decimating FIR. This trunk applies stride 2 three times, so the deepest feature map lives at 1/8 resolution: each position there summarizes an 8×8 neighborhood (and, through the stacked kernels, sees far wider). That pyramid — full-res edges at the bottom, coarse "what is this region" at the top — is the standard shape of a vision net. Hold onto the 1/8 number: chapters 04 and 05 are both about what that decimation costs you.

Do the parameter bookkeeping like chapter 02 of the pilot's notebook: a 3×3 conv from Cin to Cout channels costs 9·Cin·Cout + Cout numbers. The trunk's six layers (3→8→16→16→24→24→32) total 19,344; the decoder that paints answers back at full resolution adds ~8,500; about 28,000 all in. One more term you'll meet: batch normalization ("BN") re-centers and re-scales each channel during training to keep the optimization well-conditioned — and because it's affine, at export it folds into the neighboring conv weights like constant-folding a linear stage. The shipped net has no BN; it trained with it and absorbed it.

Finally the task itself: segmentation means per-pixel classification — the output isn't one label but a full-resolution map of 5 scores per pixel (per-pixel logits; argmax paints the overlay). Same classifier machinery as ever, evaluated everywhere at once. You can now read the deep dive.

For the data scientists

A deliberately boring fully-convolutional net, designed backward from what a simple integer accelerator kernel can execute. The ops allow-list is the real spec: 3×3 conv, 1×1 conv, stride-2 conv, 4×4 average-pool, ×2 nearest-neighbor upsample, elementwise add, ReLU. Nothing else — no concat, no attention, no fancy resize. BN trains and folds at export.

Class-map decisions that mattered: hulls differ in silhouette so the interceptor/fighter split can't lean on color alone; the hole class is the hard event-horizon disk only (the glow is background — labeling the glow would teach the net a soft, ambiguous boundary); thrust flames are ignore-class 255, excluded from the loss, because they're transient decoration neither background nor ship. Small design choices like these are where segmentation projects are quietly won or lost.

03 · The labels

A million perfect labels, free.

Vision projects usually die in labeling: humans drawing boxes, arguing over edges, at dollars per image. This one has a loophole — the game draws its own answers. The lab's renderer draws every entity twice from the same geometry: once shaded into the pretty frame, once flat into an answer sheet where each pixel carries its true class. No labelers, no label review, no licensing. The only human check is looking at a contact sheet to confirm the renderer itself draws correctly.

The training diet is seeded and synthetic — random legal scenes, plus about a third real recorded duels for realistic orbits and firefights — and one entire recorded duel was locked away untouched. That held-out duel is the film in chapter 01.

For the learners

Unlike the pilot (reinforcement learning, no right answers), the eye is trained supervised — the setting you remember: inputs paired with ground-truth targets, minimize a loss. Everything therefore hangs on where the targets come from and whether you can trust them. Here the renderer is the labeler: frame and answer-sheet are two projections of the same scene graph, so labels are pixel-perfect by construction. There is no label noise term in this experiment at all — which is exactly what makes it a clean teaching instrument; every error you'll meet in chapters 04–05 is genuinely the model's.

The discipline vocabulary: the train / validation / test split exists because a model must be judged on data it never fit ("validation" steers design choices; "test" is touched last, once). Leakage is any path by which test information sneaks into training — the silent killer of ML results, the equivalent of qualifying a part on the same units you tuned it with. The defense here is structural: every scene is generated from a seed, and the splits draw from disjoint seed blocks — non-overlapping by construction, not by bookkeeping. Plus one whole recorded episode held out, because random scenes can't test what correlated, duel-shaped sequences test.

The catch you should be asking about: synthetic data is too clean. A net trained on noiseless renders can learn to depend on that cleanliness — chapter 05 of the pilot's notebook called this training against a lie. The counter is domain randomization, encountered there as theory and used here for real: every training frame gets randomly jittered brightness, per-channel gain, DC offset, and additive Gaussian noise, so the net is forced to be robust across the tolerance band rather than exact to the nominal. A separate, deliberately harsher stress set probes robustness beyond the training jitter — corner-case qualification after Monte-Carlo design centering.

For the data scientists

Generator recipe: seeded random scenes (ships anywhere legal, 0–8 lasers in flight, 5% dead ships, varied star clutter) mixed ~30% with frames from recorded self-play replays, which contribute the correlations random placement can't — real orbits, close passes, laser exchanges. Splits are disjoint seed blocks (train base 1M, val 2M, test 3M); episode seed_005 is fully held out and is what the demo and the film replay. Each split ships as one compressed npz plus a manifest recording every seed — any shard is bit-reproducible.

Train-time photometric augmentation (label-preserving only): brightness gain ±15%, DC bias ±0.08, per-channel gain ±10%, Gaussian noise up to σ=8/255. The fixed stress set (its own seed) pushes harder: ±20%, ±0.12, σ=12/255. Geometry is never augmented — the renderer can just draw more geometry, which is cleaner than warping labels.

Scale: 2,857 train / 400 val frames proved sufficient — with perfect labels and a 28k-parameter model, data efficiency is not the fight it usually is. Dataset generation, training end to end (both stages, next chapters), and export together run in well under ten minutes on a laptop, all from one seed.

04 · The traps

99.9% in the lab, blind in the field.

The first version trained on small cropped tiles and aced them: 99.9% accurate. Run on full frames, it saw nothing — it called every pixel background. Not degraded: blind. The second trap was quieter: the way tiles were being labeled made the 3-pixel lasers mathematically invisible — most laser tiles didn't count as laser at all, so the net was being taught to ignore them.

Both traps share a moral the pilot's notebook already taught once: the test you pass must be the test you'll face. The training views weren't the deployment views, and the score was measuring the wrong world. Fix the world — train on full frames, label tiles by what's present in them — and the same architecture that was blind sees everything.

For the learners

Trap one is a boundary-condition bug you'd catch instantly in DSP dress. Convolving a small isolated tile forces zero-padding at its borders — every layer's output near an edge is computed against synthetic zeros. On a 32×32 tile, with a stack whose receptive field is wider than the tile, effectively every output is a border output: the net learned features of the padded view. Run the same weights across a full frame and the padding vanishes — real neighbors everywhere — and the features the net relied on simply don't occur. It's window edge effects: measure a spectrum through a 32-sample window, then apply those calibrations to a continuous stream, and wonder why nothing matches. The trained-on view and the deployed view were two different linear systems.

Trap two is aliasing — genuinely, not metaphorically. The coarse first-stage net (next chapter) makes one decision per 8×8 tile, and each tile was initially labeled by its center pixel's class. A laser is a 3-pixel streak: the probability it lands on the stride-8 lattice of center pixels is about one in seven. The signal is narrower than the sampling grid — sub-Nyquist labeling — so six of seven laser tiles were labeled "background" while containing a laser. The net did exactly what it was taught: ignore lasers. The fix is the right sampling theory too: label each tile by the rarest class present anywhere in it — an occupancy OR, not a point sample — which guarantees every entity lights at least one tile.

The meta-lesson generalizes past ML: both traps produced excellent scores. Nothing in the training loop can warn you that the benchmark measures the wrong thing — the 99.9% was real, on a test set constructed with the same flaw. The only defense is an end-to-end check in deployment conditions, which is why every stage of this project gates on full-frame held-out evaluation before proceeding. Trust benches you've correlated, twice over.

For the data scientists

Lesson one, verbatim from the notebook: patches ≠ full frames. Training classified isolated 32×32 patches (99.9% val accuracy); run fully-convolutionally the model collapsed to all-background. Isolated patches zero-pad every conv at their borders; full-frame windows see real neighbors; and the trunk's receptive field exceeds 32 px, so the two views are not equivalent — the patch statistics the net fit don't exist in deployment. Fix: dense-coarse training on full frames, identical architecture, one decision per stride-8 cell against a coarse label grid.

Lesson two: label cells by presence, not center pixel. Center-pixel labeling makes a 3-px laser land on the stride-8 center lattice ~1 time in 7 — undetectable by construction, no amount of training fixes a label scheme that erases the class. Cells are labeled by the rarest class present in the tile (priority: laser > ships > hole > background), guaranteeing every entity lights ≥1 cell. The coarse stage then gates on per-class recall, not overall accuracy — overall accuracy is 92% background and lies fluently.

Result after both fixes, still at 1/8 resolution: 99.6% cell accuracy, worst class 96.2%. That coarse "heatmap" stage shipped as milestone 1 and survives in the demo as a view mode — watching the net see in 8-pixel tiles makes the next chapter's problem visceral.

05 · The plateau

A ±1-pixel halo was worth forty points.

Getting from "one verdict per 8×8 tile" to "a verdict for every pixel" stalled hard. The upgrade path — let the net paint at full resolution by scaling its coarse understanding back up — plateaued at a score of about 0.59 on the small objects, no matter how long it trained. The reason is honest arithmetic: after squinting down to 1/8 resolution, the information about exactly which pixel an 18-pixel ship hull covers is gone. The net knew what was there and roughly where; it could not know where the edges were.

The fix cost 1,180 parameters — four percent of the model. Give the decoder shortcut wires from the trunk's earlier, sharper layers, so full-resolution edge detail flows directly to the painting stage instead of dying in the squeeze. The small-object score jumped from 0.585 to 0.973. On objects that small, a one-pixel halo of error is the whole score.

For the learners

First, the metric, because every number here uses it: IoU (intersection-over-union) for a class is overlap ÷ union of predicted and true pixel sets — 1.0 is perfect, and it punishes both kinds of error symmetrically. Small objects make IoU brutal: an 18-px hull misdrawn by a 1-px ring loses tens of points, because the ring is a large fraction of a small area. That's the whole plateau in one sentence: an ×8 upsample can only place edges to ±4 px, and ±4 px on an 18-px object caps IoU near 0.6. The decoder wasn't undertrained — it was information-limited. No optimizer recovers what decimation destroyed; you know this theorem.

The fix is the field's standard answer, worth knowing by name: skip connections. The trunk still exists at 1/1, 1/2, and 1/4 resolution in its early layers — the sharp spatial detail isn't gone from the network, only from its deepest map. So the decoder, at each ×2 upsample, adds in a (1×1-projected) copy of the matching-resolution trunk layer: coarse semantics from above, crisp localization from the side. Feedforward bypass around a bandwidth-limited stage. This is the U-Net idea, the backbone of a decade of segmentation work (and of biomedical imaging in particular). One engineering nuance from this lab: the skips are additive — elementwise adds, not the textbook channel-concatenation — deliberately, because an add stays trivial in 8-bit integer arithmetic (chapter 06) while concat complicates scales. Design for quantization before quantizing.

Second theme of the chapter: class weighting, the knob for imbalanced data. Lasers are a few thousandths of the pixels, so an unweighted loss barely notices them; weighting laser errors up makes the net eager — high recall (misses almost nothing) at low precision (paints lasers a bit too generously, ~2× bloom). That trade is one knob on one curve, and where to sit on it is a product decision, not a math one: in a live demo, a missed laser reads as blindness while a slightly fat laser reads as glow. The lab chose recall, on purpose, and wrote the reason down. Chapter 06's calibration decision rhymes with this one.

For the data scientists

Lesson four: the no-skip decoder (NN-upsample + 3×3 stacks from the /8 map alone) plateaued at val IoU ~0.585 interceptor / ~0.60 laser across a wide LR/schedule sweep — a ±1-px ring on an 18-px hull costs exactly the missing IoU; the failure is representational, not optimization. Additive 1×1-projected skips from trunk stages at /4, /2, /1 (+1,180 params) took min-class val IoU 0.585 → 0.973. Additive rather than concat keeps the decoder int8-clean: elementwise adds requantize to the sum tensor's scale; no concat means no scale-reconciliation across channel groups.

Lesson three: inverse-sqrt class-frequency weights put laser at precision 0.46 / recall 0.99 on held-out (≈2× spatial bloom); softer cbrt weights traded to precision 0.62 / recall 0.90 — rejected, because a missed laser reads worse in the demo than a confidence-dimmed bloom, and the real precision fix is the per-pixel decoder anyway (post-skip laser: val IoU 0.998).

Final dense numbers (M2, worst class = interceptor): val min IoU 0.9733, held-out episode 0.9652, stress set 0.9653 — gates were 0.70/0.60/0.50, all cleared with margin. Training: dense stage inits from the coarse stage's weights (~70 s), then 14 epochs AdamW + cosine, ~4 min total on a laptop, seed 20260708 end to end.

06 · Eight bits

The outliers were the lasers.

The trained net thinks in 32-bit floating point. Cheap silicon thinks in 8-bit integers. Quantization is the conversion — and it's a bargain: a quarter the memory, integer math instead of floating point, and if you do it right, nobody can tell. The film's alternating segment is that claim, live: float and int8, two pixels different out of 61,440.

The one real decision hiding in "do it right" made the best story in the project. Standard practice says: when choosing each signal's numeric range, clip the rarest, brightest extremes — outliers waste your precious 256 levels. Here, standard practice failed one class: the lasers. Because on these clean synthetic frames the rare bright spikes aren't noise — the rare bright activations are the lasers. Clip them and the eye goes blind to exactly the thing it fought hardest to see. Keep the full range and everything passes. Know your signal before you trust a heuristic about it.

For the learners

This chapter is your home turf: quantization is fixed-point datapath design wearing ML clothes. The scheme, in your vocabulary: every weight and every activation gets mapped to int8 by a scale factor — choose full-scale F for a signal, code it as round(127·x/F). Choosing F is an ADC front-end decision, and it's the entire game: too large and your LSB is coarse (quantization noise); too small and you clip. Weights are known at export, so each output channel gets its own exact scale (per-channel). Activations depend on the input, so you estimate their ranges empirically by streaming calibration frames through the float net and recording observed extremes — characterizing signal statistics to set the ADC range. Multiply int8×int8 and accumulate in int32 — the classic wide accumulator, exactly your MAC datapath discipline — then requantize the sum back to int8 through a precomputed per-channel multiplier. Everything symmetric around zero (no offset terms — this scheme carries no zero-points), which is what kept chapter 05's additive skips trivial: adds happen in integer domain and requantize once.

The calibration decision: the textbook heuristic sets each activation's F at the 99.9th percentile of observed magnitude — "spend no dynamic range on outliers," which on natural images (sensor noise, sun glints) is usually right. On synthetic frames it's precisely wrong, and the reason is signal statistics: these images are nearly binary — flat dark space, a few small bright objects — and the laser-detecting channels fire rarely and hard. Their top 0.1% of activations isn't a noise tail; it's the entire signal. A percentile clip is a crest-factor assumption, and lasers have the crest factor of, well, a laser. Max calibration (F = the true observed maximum, no clipping) costs a little resolution everywhere, and here that cost is absorbable — the net's decisions have huge margins. Laser accuracy under percentile clipping: fails the gate. Under max: identical to float.

Why is such a crude scheme (post-training, no fine-tuning — the field calls it PTQ, post-training quantization) sufficient? Margin. A well-trained classifier's argmax decisions sit far from its decision boundaries; int8's ~0.4% worst-case rounding perturbs logits far less than the typical inter-class gap. When PTQ isn't enough — huge models, tighter formats — the field retrains with quantization simulated in the loop (QAT, quantization-aware training). Rule of thumb worth exporting: try the cheap thing first and measure per-class; aggregate metrics hide exactly the small, rare classes that clipping kills.

For the data scientists

Scheme: symmetric per-out-channel int8 weights; symmetric per-tensor activations; int32 bias and accumulators; no zero-points anywhere; per-channel requantize multipliers recorded in the export (a hardware target swaps them for fixed-point multiplier+shift pairs). Additive skips requantize to the sum tensor's scale — plain integer adds. The integer path is defined end to end in integer arithmetic, which makes it bit-exact reproducible on any platform — the bundle's verifier (next chapter) checks the integer path byte-for-byte, not approximately.

Lesson five: 99.9-percentile activation calibration failed the int8 gate on the small classes (laser int8-vs-float IoU 0.963, below the 0.98 gate) — the rare bright activations a percentile clips are the lasers. Max calibration passed everything, laser IoU vs float = 1.000. On near-binary synthetic frames there is no outlier noise to trim; clipping only destroys signal. The percentile constant now sits in the code at 100.0 with the lesson documented above it.

Acceptance, measured: ≥99.99% per-pixel argmax agreement vs float; per-class IoU vs float ≥0.99 (gate 0.98); demo A/B on a busy held-out frame differs by 2 of 61,440 overlay pixels. PTQ only — no QAT needed at these margins.

07 · The handoff

What ships is a folder anyone can check.

The experiment's product isn't a demo — it's a frozen bundle: the trained numbers (float and int8), a reference implementation of the math in plain code short enough to read, and a set of golden vectors — known inputs with their exact recorded outputs. Anyone porting the eye to new hardware re-implements the arithmetic on their target, runs the same inputs, and checks against the goldens. One command, one dependency, pass or fail.

Two properties make the folder trustworthy. It's relocatable — copy it anywhere, it still verifies; it doesn't need the lab. And it's reproducible — the entire experiment re-runs from one seed, dataset through training through export, and lands on the same file, bit for bit. The chapter-01 film honors the same contract: every painted pixel you watched came from this folder through the reference implementation, not from the training framework.

For the learners

You've shipped this folder before — it's a characterization handoff. The reference implementation is the executable spec (the golden model, in the verification-engineering sense); the golden vectors are your ATE test vectors: stimulus plus expected response, recorded once, checked forever. A port that matches the goldens is correct by the contract's definition, no argument about methodology required. What ML adds is a subtlety about tolerances, and it's worth internalizing: the float gate can't demand bit-equality (float summation order legitimately differs across BLAS libraries and hardware — the same value-vs-implementation distinction as analog tolerance bands), so it specifies a numeric tolerance plus a decision-level check: do all 61,440 argmaxes agree. The int8 gate is stricter than the float one: because chapter 06 defined the integer path entirely in integer arithmetic, there is no summation-order ambiguity — the correct output is a specific string of bytes, and the verifier compares byte-for-byte. Determinism is a designed-in property, and it was bought back in chapter 05 (additive skips) before quantization ever ran.

The reproducibility claim is the other half. One seed drives scene generation, splits, initialization, and training order, and re-running the pipeline reproduces the shipped bundle bit-for-bit. For a reader from hardware this sounds unremarkable — of course the build rebuilds — but in ML it's rare enough to be a differentiator: nondeterministic GPU kernels, undocumented data filtering, and "the checkpoint we happened to keep" are endemic. A bit-reproducible experiment is falsifiable end to end: every number in this notebook can be regenerated or caught wrong. That's the standard worth importing from your field into this one.

Where you'd go next, and where this notebook deliberately stops: the bundle describes what a port must compute, never how — no target, no toolchain, no vendor. That one-way fence keeps the public artifact universal: any student with a microcontroller, an FPGA, or a GPU shader can port the eye and prove their port against the same goldens. The two experiments on this site are the two halves of an edge autonomy stack — policy that decides (the pilot) and perception that sees (the eye) — each one shippable as a folder of checkable arithmetic.

For the data scientists

Bundle contents: frozen float + int8 models (dense JSON — plain arrays, no framework), a pure-numpy reference forward (~200 lines, numpy is the verifier's only dependency), eight seeded golden vectors, and a one-command verifier. Verification ladder, in order: float gate — recomputed logits within 1e-4 of goldens (measured deviation ~2.4e-05) and 100% per-pixel argmax agreement; int8 gate — the integer path reproduces stored outputs bit-exactly (int8 weights, int32 accumulators — deterministic on any platform), plus ≥99% argmax agreement vs float and per-class IoU ≥0.98 vs float. Float first, always: quantization error is only meaningful against a float reference you've already proven.

Relocatable: copy the directory anywhere, run the verifier from a bare copy — pass. Reproducible: the full pipeline (generate → train coarse → train dense → export → quantize → bundle) re-run from seed 20260708 reproduces the bundle bit-for-bit. A downstream port replaces the reference forward with its own implementation and runs the same script against the same goldens; nothing flows back upstream. The consumer is described only as what it is from here: a hardware port, on whatever tiny accelerator it targets.
